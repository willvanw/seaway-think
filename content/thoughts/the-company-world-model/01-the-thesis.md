Every company operates on a set of assumptions about how the world works. Most of the time, these assumptions are implicit — buried in strategy decks, embedded in pricing models, whispered in executive hallways. They rarely get surfaced, tested, or articulated as a coherent whole.

This is a problem. Because when your assumptions are implicit, you can't interrogate them. You can't update them when the world shifts. You can't align a growing organization around them. And you definitely can't compound your learning over time.

## What is a world model?

A world model is the explicit, structured set of beliefs a company holds about how its environment works — and how that environment is changing. It answers questions like:

- What do our customers actually value, and how is that shifting?
- What are the structural forces shaping our industry over the next decade?
- Where are we betting on consensus being wrong?
- What has to be true for our strategy to work?

It's not a strategy document. Strategy is what you *do*. A world model is what you *believe* — the foundation on which strategy is built.

## Why now?

The rate of environmental change has outpaced the rate at which most organizations update their beliefs. The companies that will win the next decade aren't the ones with the best strategy — they're the ones with the fastest, most accurate model of reality.

A world model gives you three things:

**Clarity.** When everyone shares an explicit model of how the world works, alignment becomes structural rather than political. Debates shift from "what should we do?" to "what do we believe?" — which is a far more productive question.

**Speed.** New information has a place to land. Instead of relitigating first principles in every meeting, you update the model and let the implications cascade.

**Compounding.** Every observation, every market signal, every customer conversation becomes an input that makes the model more accurate over time. This is the real edge — not any single insight, but the rate at which your understanding improves.

This series documents a line of thinking about how to build, maintain, and operationalize company world models. It's not a finished framework — it's thinking in progress. Each piece builds on the last, and the goal is to arrive at something practical enough to implement and rigorous enough to trust.
