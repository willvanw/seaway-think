title: "Information Pipeline Problem"
subtitle: "Every organization is an information pipeline. Most just don't know it yet."
date: "2026-04-03"

---

In 2023, the global management consulting industry crossed $374 billion in annual revenue. That's more than the GDP of Colombia. More than Nike, Starbucks, and Goldman Sachs combined. And the vast majority of that money pays for a single activity that no one names correctly.

It's not strategy. It's not transformation. It's not digital, or operational, or organizational. Those are the labels on the invoice. The actual work — the thing a consulting team does when it enters your building and starts booking conference rooms — is building an information pipeline.

They interview your people. They sit in your meetings. They read your documents, your Slack channels, your project trackers. They aggregate context that exists in dozens of heads across dozens of teams, synthesize it into a picture, and deliver that picture to the people who need it most — usually the ones at the top who can see the least.

The entire engagement is an act of information routing. Context goes in. A PowerPoint comes out. The client pays half a million dollars. The consultants leave. And within six months, the picture is stale, because the organization that generated the original context never stopped changing.

This is the information pipeline problem. And it sits at the center of how most large organizations actually operate.

---

Consider what happens inside a company of 2,000 people when the CEO asks a simple question: "Why is our product launch delayed?"

The question travels down. Chief of Staff asks the VP of Product. The VP asks three directors. The directors ask their leads. The leads check with the engineers, the designers, the vendor team, legal. Context flows back up through each layer, getting compressed, editorialized, and filtered at every stop.

By the time the answer reaches the CEO, it has passed through five or six humans, each applying their own frame. The process took two weeks. The answer is a partial truth shaped by organizational politics.

This is not a failure of the people. It's a failure of the architecture. The hierarchy is doing exactly what it was designed to do: route information through layers of human judgment with defined span of control. The problem is that the architecture was designed for an era when human relay was the only option. That era ended, and almost no one noticed.

---

Andy Jassy calls them "preliminary meetings for preliminary meetings of decision-making meetings." Brian Chesky calls it "meetings about meetings, where metrics and strategic priorities are the only things that bind the company together." Tobi Lütke says bureaucracy is "a road to evil in businesses" and describes how "sediment layers build up over time to create outcomes nobody intended, happening so incrementally that nobody notices."

These aren't fringe operators complaining about overhead. These are the CEOs of Amazon, Airbnb, and Shopify — companies worth a combined $3 trillion — all independently naming the same structural failure.

And they're all moving to fix it.

Jassy launched a campaign to increase Amazon's IC-to-manager ratio by 15% and created a "Bureaucracy Mailbox" that received 1,500 employee submissions — leading to changes in 455 specific processes. Bayer's CEO eliminated traditional decision-making hierarchies entirely, replacing them with autonomous teams on 90-day cycles. Jack Dorsey cut 40% of Block's workforce and replaced middle management with AI coordination. Shopify now requires teams to prove AI can't do a job before requesting new headcount.

The pattern is unmistakable. The people running the world's most valuable companies have concluded that their own organizational structures are the bottleneck. Not the market. Not the product. Not the competition. The hierarchy itself.

---

The data confirms what the CEOs are feeling.

Asana's Anatomy of Work study found that employees spend 60% of their workday on coordination — communicating about tasks, hunting for documents, managing shifting priorities. Only 27% goes to the work they were actually hired to do. Strategic planning gets 13%.

Individual contributors now spend 3.7 hours per week in meetings they consider unproductive — a 118% increase since 2019. Executives spend 5.3 hours. CEOs average 37 meetings per week, consuming roughly two-thirds of their waking work hours.

This is not a productivity problem. This is a structural tax. The organization spends more energy routing information about the work than doing the work itself.

And when the internal pipeline fails — when the routing is too slow, too lossy, too political — companies hire external pipelines. They call McKinsey. They bring in Deloitte. They pay Bain $3,000 a day per consultant to do the thing their own hierarchy was supposed to do: move context from where it exists to where it's needed.

---

Management consulting as an industry was built on this failure. Not deliberately, but inevitably.

In 1959, McKinsey's Gilbert Clee and Alfred di Scipio published "Creating a World Enterprise" in the Harvard Business Review, providing the intellectual framework for matrix organizations that combined functional specialties with divisional units. McKinsey helped Shell, GE, and dozens of others implement these structures. They were good at it. They were also, unknowingly, creating the conditions for their own future revenue.

Matrix organizations are more complex than simple hierarchies. More complexity means more coordination overhead. More coordination overhead means more demand for external help navigating it. The consulting industry didn't just advise companies on organizational design — it designed organizations that would need more consulting.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's an incentive structure. And the incentive structure runs on a single economic mechanism: the billable hour.

A management consulting engagement is priced on time, not outcomes. The firm sends smart people into your building. They spend weeks learning your context. They synthesize what they find. They present recommendations. Whether those recommendations work — whether decision cycle times actually decrease, whether teams actually align, whether the information pipeline actually improves — is largely beside the point. The invoice is for the hours, not the result.

Sixty-seven percent of consulting buyers now say they prefer outcome-based or fixed-fee arrangements. Only 25% of McKinsey's fees are outcome-linked. The market wants something different from what the industry sells.

---

So why hasn't this changed?

Because until recently, there was no alternative to human-powered information routing. A manager's job — the actual job, stripped of title and ceremony — is to know what's happening across their span of control and relay that context to the people above and below them. No technology before now could do that. Email made messages faster but didn't aggregate context. Slack made communication more visible but created noise instead of signal. Dashboards displayed metrics but couldn't explain why the metrics moved.

AI changes the equation in a way that none of those tools did.

A modern AI system can ingest the same sources a consulting team would: meeting transcripts, Slack conversations, project management data, documents, email threads, org charts. It can map how information actually flows — where context travels, where it gets stuck, which decisions are bottlenecked, which teams are misaligned. And it can do this continuously, not as a one-time engagement.

The difference is not speed, although it's faster. The difference is that the picture never goes stale. A consulting engagement produces a snapshot. An intelligence layer produces a living model.

---

This is what Jack Dorsey described when he wrote about replacing hierarchy with intelligence. Not giving everyone a copilot. Not making the existing structure slightly more efficient. Replacing the coordination substrate itself.

In Dorsey's framing, a company needs two world models. A company world model — a continuously updated understanding of its own operations, decisions, progress, and problems. And a customer world model — a per-customer understanding built from proprietary signal. Together, they form the foundation for what he calls an intelligence layer: a system that composes capabilities into solutions without requiring human relay at every step.

The consulting industry is what happens when the company world model doesn't exist. When the only way to build that picture is to hire fifty people at $300 an hour to interview your employees and read your Slack channels for six weeks. The $374 billion isn't paying for strategy. It's paying for the absence of a system that should have existed all along.

---

The implications compress into three claims.

First, the coordination tax and the consulting spend are the same problem. Companies pay for broken information routing twice — once internally through meetings, approval chains, and management overhead, and once externally through consultants, lawyers, and recruiters who exist partly because internal coordination is too slow to solve problems in-house. Any solution that addresses one without the other is solving half the problem.

Second, outcome-based pricing is not just a preference — it's a structural inevitability. When the deliverable shifts from "hours of smart people in your building" to "measurable reduction in coordination overhead," the entire pricing model inverts. Intercom already prices its AI agents at $0.99 per resolution and is approaching $100 million in ARR. The pattern will reach consulting. The firms that resist it will lose to the systems that embrace it.

Third, the company that builds the intelligence layer — the system that replaces the information pipeline with a continuously learning model — captures both the internal coordination budget and the external consulting budget simultaneously. That's not a product. That's a new category.

---

There's a question worth asking about any industry that crosses $374 billion: what is this money actually buying?

In management consulting, the honest answer is context aggregation and information routing. The same functions that hierarchy was invented to perform. The same functions that CEOs of the world's most valuable companies are now publicly declaring broken. The same functions that, for the first time in two thousand years of organizational design, a non-human system can perform continuously, at scale, without the information loss that comes from passing context through five layers of human judgment.

The information pipeline problem is not new. It's as old as the Roman contubernium, as old as the Prussian General Staff, as old as McCallum's first org chart sketched for the Erie Railroad in 1855. What's new is that the problem is now solvable by something other than adding more people to the chain.

The $374 billion question is simple: what happens to an industry built on routing information through humans, when humans are no longer the only option?

The answer will arrive faster than the industry expects.
